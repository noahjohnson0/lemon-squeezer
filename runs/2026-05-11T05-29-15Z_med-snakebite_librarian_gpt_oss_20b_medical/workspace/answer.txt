**Field‑Treatment Plan for Suspected Rattlesnake Bite**

1. **Immediate actions** – Keep the bitten limb still and as flat as possible; do NOT apply a tight tourniquet, pressure bandage, or elevate the limb above heart level (Snakebite.md).  Wash the bite site gently with soap and water if available; keep the victim calm and restrict movement (Snakebite.md).

2. **What NOT to do** –
   • Do not try to suck the venom out – it can introduce bacteria and is ineffective (Snakebite.md).  
   • Do not cut or incise the wound – this can spread venom and increase infection risk (Snakebite.md).  
   • Do not place a tourniquet or tight compression bandage – it can cause tissue ischemia and worsen local injury (Snakebite.md).

3. **Marking bite progression** – Measure the distance from the puncture to the edge of swelling every 15–30 minutes, note changes in color (redness, blisters, bruising), and record any new symptoms such as increasing pain or numbness (Snakebite.md).

4. **Transport priority** – Arrange emergency transport to the nearest hospital immediately; do not wait for symptoms to worsen (Snakebite.md).  A 2‑hour drive is an emergency, so expedite vehicle or call for air‑lift if available (Snakebite.md).

5. **Information for the hospital** – Tell staff: suspected rattlesnake, bite location (lower right calf), time since bite (≈30 min), first‑aid performed (wash, no suction/incision/tourniquet), current symptoms (pain, swelling, nausea), and that the victim is conscious and anxious (Snakebite.md).

6. **Warning signs en route** – Monitor for:
   • Increasing swelling or spreading redness
   • Progressive pain or numbness
   • Hypotension, tachycardia, or bradycardia
   • Breathlessness or difficulty speaking (neuro‑toxicity signs)
   • New bleeding (from gums, nose, or wounds)
   • Altered mental status (confusion, lethargy) (Snakebite.md).

All statements are derived directly from the *Snakebite.md* reference. If any symptom suddenly worsens, seek immediate emergency care even before arriving at the hospital.
